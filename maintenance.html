Home Maintenance Checklist

What Regular Maintenance Should I Do On My House?

Home maintenance may be fundamentally similar across all properties in every climate, but the finer details of your home may be unique to even those of your neighbors in the same subdivision. With that said, whether you are a homeowner of a sprawling Tudor-style home or a cozy cottage, or even if you are an older adult looking into aging in place home modifications, there are some home maintenance tasks that should be a part of your monthly routine if they aren't already.

Monthly Home Maintenance

HVAC filters are some of the hardest workers in your home, cleaning your air while ensuring your air conditioning and heating system is operating at its best. While you should check your HVAC filter every month, some filters can go 60 days without being replaced. However, if record high (or low) temperatures have seen you put your HVAC system through the wringer, your HVAC filter’s lifespan may shorten. While you’re at it, be sure that all vents are clean and unobstructed so that your new filter doesn’t inherit the old filter’s mess!

Another monthly maintenance task to incorporate into your routine is a regular test of your home’s safety mechanisms. In particular, be sure to test your home’s smoke detector and carbon monoxide detector to ensure all batteries are operational. While this can be done every quarter, we recommend checking your alarms more frequently than absolutely necessary. And, considering hundreds of homeowners die from carbon monoxide poisoning every year, this test is a key component of your home maintenance checklist.

Our last recommendation for monthly home maintenance is one that is often overlooked: cleaning your garbage disposal. While the inner machinations of a garbage disposal are not fully known by many homeowners (where does the food go?) those who have had to deep clean one know how important it is to regularly check them. Contrary to popular belief, the food that goes down a disposal does not vanish from thin air — rather, it accumulates, cultivates odor-causing bacteria, and can even begin to grow mold. Homeowners should exercise caution when cleaning their disposal, but the process can be done in less than an hour and should be done well before it becomes noticeable.

Apart from monthly maintenance, there are larger tasks that will have to be done quarterly as well. Whether you want to complete these separate from your season-specific home maintenance tasks (outlined below) or in one fell swoop, be sure that these are included in your home maintenance checklist.

Quarterly Home Maintenance

While smoke and carbon monoxide detectors should be checked every month or two, other household safety equipment like fire extinguishers can be inspected quarterly. To make sure your fire extinguisher is primed for action, inspect its pressure gauges as part of your quarterly home maintenance checklist.

Like cleaning your garbage disposal, frequent tests of your garage door’s auto-reverse function should also be a part of your quarterly home maintenance checklist. While not all garage door openers have this function, all doors manufactured after 1993 are required to have it by law. If you’re not sure if your garage door is equipped with this, now is the perfect time to find out! After making sure that your sensors are in their proper place, test this potentially life-saving function by doing the following:

  • Start by closing your garage door, and pulling the manual release mechanism so that you can smoothly lift the door to about three feet off the ground.
  • If the garage door continues to open, the door is likely unbalanced and will need an adjustment.
  • After determining the door is balanced, disengage the manual operation and fully open the door.
  • Place a small block of wood on the floor of your garage beneath the garage door threshold, and use the door’s controller to close the door.
  • If the door reverses upon contact with the block, your door has passed the test. If not, this function will need to be repaired.

While this is not a costly repair, home repair costs can accumulate — and we’ll discuss how to cover these costs later on. If not repaired, faulty garage doors can be harmful to people, pets, and objects.

Last, on your quarterly home maintenance checklist, be sure to test your home’s electrical outlets every three months or so. Open up your home’s breaker box and press the “test” button on the ground-fault circuit-interrupter (GFCI) breaker. Then flip the breaker handle off and back on again. If your circuit does not trip after pressing the test button, you will need to replace your GFCI outlet.

What Parts of My House Need Regular Maintenance?

While each part of your home won’t need regular maintenance, your home maintenance checklist can be broken down by seasonal tasks and further divided according to each part of your home. Assuming you live in a four-season climate, the following seasonal home maintenance checklist paired with the above tests can help you prioritize maintenance and repairs.

Fall Outdoor

If each season of your home maintenance checklist had a theme, the fall season would be preparation. For homeowners who live in climates where winters are white and windy, your home checklist should consist mostly of tasks that will prepare your home for whatever the season throws at it.

  • Take down both window screens and window unit air conditioners and store for the winter to prevent damage.
  • Clear leaves and debris from outdoor water features like gutters.
  • Protect outdoor faucets by covering them with foam blocks.
  • Install storm doors and storm windows if needed.

Fall Indoor

In addition to your outdoor home maintenance tasks, add the following indoor checks to your seasonal home maintenance checklist:

  • Have your furnace tested by a professional.
  • Reverse ceiling fans so that they’re spinning in the opposite direction. Considering warm air rises, this will help distribute any accumulated heat — and lower your heating bill.
  • Winterize your central AC system to prevent damage.
  • Identify indoor areas which might benefit from a winter remodel.

Winter Outdoor

Depending on the harshness of the elements, you may not be able to tackle many outdoor home maintenance tasks during the winter. Still, the following should be done each winter to limit the damage to your home.

  • During the warmer parts of the day - after most potential ice has melted, use a sturdy ladder to clean gutters and check the roof for ice buildup. To the best of your ability, break up any ice dams you find along the way.
  • Ensure that the ground near your home slopes away from the home, limiting the chances of indoor flooding.
  • Look for areas where water is pooling — this can be a sign you need a new drainage system.

Winter Indoor

The weather outside is frightful, so you can spend the majority of the winter season checking items on your indoor home maintenance checklist!

  • Deep clean your garage, basement, and other areas which need attention.
  • Tackle the remodeling projects you outlined in the fall. These can include retiling, renovating a bathroom, or anything else you see fit. However, if you’re expecting holiday visitors, plan accordingly!
  • From the warmth and comfort of your home, pay close attention to fluctuations in electrical service. If you notice a downed powerline due to a winter storm or fallen limbs, call your local utility company.

Spring Outdoor

Spring can be hit-or-miss, and while the weather may be warmer you may want to wait until summer to tackle any ambitious outdoor projects. In the meantime:

  • Reinstall screens and window AC units, and remove your storm doors. Depending on your climate, you may want to wait until later in the season to do so.
  • Analyze any damage to the exterior of your home. If you see siding which may need replacing, shingles that incurred damage, or bent-up vents, you may have your summer maintenance cut out for you.
  • Prepare for summer storms by trimming any potentially harmful branches. If you notice any limbs that may pose a threat to power lines, call your local utility company.

Spring Indoor

Indoor tasks will be minimal during the spring, so this is a great opportunity for you to finish up your winter renovation projects, give your house a deep spring clean, and plan (and budget) for more significant summer tasks.

  • Reverse your ceiling fan so that it spins in its original direction and circulates cool air down.

Summer Outdoor

Whereas fall’s theme was preparation, summer’s is ambition. Warmer temperatures and longer days mean more opportunities to check major items off your home maintenance checklist, so use this time wisely.

  • If your siding or shingles were damaged during the winter, use this time to make repairs and replacements.
  • In the event that your outdoor drainage system was revealed to be sub-bar, upgrade your system to prevent future flooding.
  • Use the summer season to give your home a facelift — now is the perfect time to fix exterior paint, roof replacements, and additions.

Summer Indoor

Is there anything you’ve missed this year? This is your home maintenance checklist, after all — so if the extra sunlight illuminates any tasks that need to be taken care of, now is your time. However, considering the more involved outdoor tasks that the season brings, we won’t blame you for adding “relax in the air conditioning” to your summer checklist.
